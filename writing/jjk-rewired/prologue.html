Part 0 The Prologue

Silence before the Storm

← Jujutsu Kaisen: Rewired

The mountains around Okutama were quiet in a way that made small sounds feel larger than they were—the buzz of a distant power line, the soft crunch of gravel beneath his shoes, the faint static that lingered at the tips of Kenji Raijima’s fingers even when he stood completely still. He paused outside his apartment door, taking a deep breath, listening to the afternoon air as if it might explain something to him. It never did. It rarely explained anything. But the silence here was predictable, and predictability, to Kenji, felt close enough to safety.

        Upon opening the door, a small feline walked swiftly in front of him without hesitation, only pausing in the middle of the hallway to lick her leg. Not that she needed an invitation. Kenji knew who she was. He smiled, seeing her quickly and comfortably enter the apartment.

        “Kuro. It’s nice to see you.”

        She let out a small chirp in response, walking back over to him and rubbing her fur against his leg shortly after. Kenji, tired, walked over to the couch and flopped. With every step he took along the way, she followed closely. And as soon as he lay down, she was immediately on top of his chest, purring with every movement she made. It was something so comforting, so quiet in his noisy world. She quickly got comfortable, too, forming a fluffy loaf on top of him. He gave her pets and scratches all over, but her favorite place to be scratched was definitely behind her ears, and he made sure to do that often.

        As the afternoon progressed, the cloudy sky that was present before had now evolved into a slight drizzle. The droplets that pitter-pattered against the roof above them provided a consistent noise, giving the situation an even more soothing ambience. This is what felt right, what felt in tune with his life. A few deep breaths later, Kenji reached for his phone and pulled it out, curious to see if he got any notifications. To his expectation, there weren’t any. So, he set the phone face down on his stomach and stared at the ceiling for a while. Kuro rose and fell with each breath he took, and he found himself unconsciously matching her rhythm—slower, steadier, until the rain and her purring were the only sounds that existed. His eyes grew heavy. He let them close. He didn't mean to fall asleep. He rarely did on purpose.

When he opened his eyes again, the room was darker. The drizzle outside had deepened into something more insistent, drumming harder against the window than before. Kuro was no longer on his chest. He sat up slightly, scanning the room in that slow, half-awake way, until he found her—perched on the windowsill, staring out at the street below with her tail flicking in uneven intervals.

Kenji watched her. She only did that when something caught her attention.

He pushed himself upright and rubbed his face, trying to shake the sleep off. "What is it?" he asked, though not really expecting an answer. He crossed the room and stood beside her, following her gaze down to the street. The rain was heavier now, slicking the road beneath the amber wash of a single streetlamp. Nothing looked unusual. A few parked cars. A coin laundry with its lights still on across the road. An empty bench.

He was about to turn away when the streetlamp flickered. Then went out.

Kenji went still. The static at his fingers, that quiet background hum he'd long since learned to ignore, sharpened suddenly—not painful, just present in a way it usually wasn't, like something had tuned the frequency higher without asking. Kuro made a low sound in her throat, something between a growl and a whimper, entirely unlike anything he'd heard from her before.

Then he heard the screaming.

He looked toward the sound and saw it—a block away, maybe more, but large enough that distance didn't make it smaller. The shape moved through the rain with an uneven, lurching weight, too large and too wrong, bending the light around it in ways that made his eyes feel like they were misfiring. Where it passed, the air thickened. He could feel it from here. Something in his chest recognized it even though his mind didn't, and what his chest said was leave.

He stepped back from the window. Kuro didn't.

"Kuro." He kept his voice low, the way you do when you don't want to be heard by the wrong thing. "Come on. Get away from there."

She stayed fixed, tail lashing, ears flat.

"Kuro."

The screaming grew. Then the sound of something structural giving way—a deep, compressive crack that rolled through the street like thunder. Kuro was off the windowsill before he could reach for her, bolting for the apartment door. Kenji was right behind her, fingers fumbling with the lock, wrenching the door open. Soon, they were both in the hallway, then the stairwell, and then the street, with the night swallowing them whole.

People were already running. Not a crowd—that scattered, individual kind of flight where everyone is alone in their panic, each person an island of adrenaline. The sound of destruction was closer now, punctuated by silences that felt worse than the noise. Kenji ran without deciding to, feet slapping the wet pavement, Kuro a dark streak at the edge of his vision. The rain made everything slick and strange. Neither of them stopped.

He didn't know how far they'd gone when his lungs gave out. They simply stopped cooperating, and his legs followed a moment later. He bent over his knees, chest heaving, each breath a small defeat. The adrenaline was still there, but it had nowhere to go now; it just sat in his throat.

"Kuro," he managed between breaths. "Just… go. Don't wait for me."

She had already turned back.

She was halfway to him when the building beside them came apart. Kenji didn’t have a chance to look up, as the sudden wave of debris engulfed the street with dust before he could pull his head up. He stumbled a bit, getting hit by a few small chunks of the building. He coughed quite harshly, and his eyes were getting dusty, too. He could only rely on his hearing right now, but even that was clouded. However, he knew there was something big, something dangerous lurking. Before he could act with proper judgement, the dust settled just enough for him see Kuro, seemingly lifeless on the pavement, her fur absorbing the rain. She was down and didn't get up, and it didn’t look like she was breathing either.

The second he saw her in that condition, his heartbeat became the only thing that felt real. He was cold, shaky, distraught, and was only just aware enough to notice the shadow that loomed over Kuro.

What he saw had no face he could hold in his mind; it didn’t look like anything natural. The figure it held was a distorted excuse for a human. Its neck was too long, too thick, its limbs had too many joints, and its skin was an unnatural blue.

In that moment, he couldn’t feel anything besides a growing energy within his soul. Something unbridled, something overpowering. It felt like a live wire dropped in standing water—immediate, indiscriminate, and terrifying. The static that had lived quietly at his fingertips his entire life surged outward through his arms and out through his hands, and the lightning that tore out of him lit the entire street white.

He didn't aim it. He didn't know how to. He just stood there in the rain, shaking, while the afterimage burned behind his eyes and the smell of ozone overwhelmed everything else. When his vision cleared enough to be useful, the thing was gone. Whether dead or simply fled, he couldn't tell. The streetlamps were still dark. Kuro was still on the ground.

He dropped to his knees beside her, hands hovering, afraid to touch her—afraid of what he'd just done, afraid of what was still crackling faintly across his knuckles in small, involuntary sparks. She blinked up at him. Then, slowly and with visible indignation, she stood, shook herself off, and walked a small circle as if to confirm she still could. Kenji exhaled something that wasn't quite a laugh and wasn't quite a sob. He didn't notice the figure standing at the far end of the street until he was already on his feet with Kuro tucked against his chest. She noticed them before he did — her ears swiveled first, then her gaze fixed on something over his shoulder.

"Wow, that was pretty powerful. Were you trying to remodel the town?"

Kenji spun around, still on edge, fists coming up before he'd made any conscious decision about it—as if he could do anything. His stature softened a moment later. The figure looked peculiar in a way that made it difficult to feel threatened. Tall, dressed in a full black uniform, hair as white as snow, and… a blindfold? The man didn't seem to be in any particular hurry. He looked around at the debris-scattered street with the air of someone appraising a mildly interesting renovation project. His hands were still in his pockets, and he tilted his head slightly as his gaze moved from the collapsed building to the scorch marks on the pavement, then to the still-glowing residue where the curse had been. He let out a low, appreciative whistle.

"Nice radius," he said, mostly to himself. Then, as an afterthought: "The street was already kind of ugly though, so."

Kenji stared at him. Kuro stared at him too, but with considerably more suspicion.

The man crouched down suddenly—with an ease that suggested his knees had never once complained about anything—and extended two fingers toward Kuro with the patient, unhurried confidence of someone who had never been scratched by a cat in their life and did not intend to start now. She regarded the fingers for a long moment, then, apparently deciding he was not worth the effort of hostility, allowed a brief sniff before turning her head away with tremendous dignity.

"Tough crowd." He stood back up and finally turned to face Kenji directly. Even with the blindfold, the attention felt specific. "You're not hurt?"

Kenji blinked. It took him a moment to understand the question was meant for him. "I—no. I don't think so."

"Good." He said it simply, like it settled something. Then he tilted his head again. "The cat?"

"She's…" Kenji glanced down at Kuro, who was methodically grooming her own shoulder with the focused air of someone pretending nothing had happened. "She seems okay."

"Cats are resilient." A pause. "So are you, apparently."

Kenji didn't know what to do with that, so he said nothing.

The man reached up and adjusted his blindfold in a gesture that seemed more habitual than necessary, then looked—or appeared to look—back out at the street. When he spoke again, the lightness in his voice was still there, but something underneath it had shifted, the way a room feels different when someone closes a window.

"What you did just now—the lightning. Has that happened before?"

"No." Then, more honestly: "Not like that."

"But there have been smaller things."

It wasn't quite a question. Kenji hesitated, then nodded.

"Thought so." The man seemed unsurprised, which was somehow more unnerving than any other reaction would have been. "You've been carrying that around for a while, then. Without any idea what it was."

Kenji said nothing. Outside of a single nod, he wasn't sure what kind of answer that deserved.

"There's a place," the man said, "where people like you go. To learn what they are. What they can do." He paused. "And what they're up against—because there's always something you're up against. That thing tonight wasn't a coincidence. It was drawn to you, the same way that kind of thing always gets drawn to people who have what you have." Another pause, lighter this time. "The technical term is cursed energy, by the way. In case you were looking for one."

Kenji looked down at his hands. The crackling had mostly stopped, but his palms still felt raw and strange, like the skin was remembering something his mind was still trying to forget. "And if I don't go?"

"Then you keep walking around with live wires in your hands and no instructions." The man shrugged, unbothered. "I'd give it a few months before something a lot bigger than tonight decides you smell interesting."

The rain was still falling. It had quieted from its earlier intensity into something lighter, almost gentle. Kuro was sitting there, looking around at things and hearing noises that weren’t a part of the current conversation.

"I'd have questions," he said finally.

"You'll have plenty of chances to ask them." For the first time, something in the man's expression moved toward what might generously be described as a smile. "My name is Satoru Gojo. I'll be in touch."

He turned and started walking in the direction he'd come from, hands back in his pockets, at a pace that suggested he had nowhere urgent to be and had perhaps never experienced urgency in any meaningful sense.

"Wait," Kenji said.

Gojo stopped but didn't turn around.

"How did you—" Kenji started, then tried again. "Were you watching the whole time?"

A pause. Then: "Get some sleep. You look terrible."

He kept walking, and the rain closed around him, and in a few moments, he was simply gone—the street quiet again, the amber glow of the one surviving streetlamp catching nothing but wet pavement and scattered debris and one boy standing in the middle of it all, a cat by his side, not entirely sure what had just happened to his life.
